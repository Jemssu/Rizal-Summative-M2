Books & Published Works

José Rizal's intellectual legacy is preserved not only through his actions but also through his writings. His works, both published during his lifetime and posthumously, continue to shape discussions on nationalism, identity, and freedom. This section serves as a flexible catalog for highlighting his books, essays, and related literature.

Noli Me Tangere (1887)

Noli Me Tangere exposed the abuses of Spanish friars and the ills of Philippine colonial society. It quickly became a revolutionary piece of literature, awakening national consciousness.

El Filibusterismo (1891)

El Filibusterismo, Rizal's sequel to Noli Me Tangere, is darker in tone, depicting social unrest and the call for reform. It reflects his evolving vision of the struggle for freedom.

Everlasting Impact

Rizal's novels, essays, and poems remain essential reading not only in the Philippines but also in global discussions on colonialism and human rights.

Song of Maria Clara

A poem found in Rizal's book Noli Me Tangere, sung by Maria Clara, which accounts for the title. This piece is filled with love for one's native land.

English Translation

Sweet are the hours in one's own Native Land,
All there is friendly o'er which the sun shines above;
Vivifying is the breeze that wafts over her fields;
Even death is gratifying and more tender is love.

Ardent kissed on a mother's lips are at play,
On her lap, upon the infant child's awakening,<
The extended arms do seek her neck to entwine,
And the eyes at each other's glimpse are smiling.

It is sweet to die in one's own Native Land,
All there is friendly o'er which the sun shines above;
And deathly is the breeze for one without
A country, without a mother and without love.

Ang Awit ni Maria Clara (Tagalog)

Kay tamis ng oras sa sariling bayan,
Kaibigan lahat ang abot ng araw,
At sampu ng simoy sa parang ay buhay,
Aliw ng panimdim pati kamatayan.

Maalab na halik ang nagsaliw-saliw
Sa labi ng inang mahal, pagkagising;
Ang pita ng bisig as siya'y yapusin,
Pati mga mata'y ngumgiti mandin.

Kung dahil sa bayan, kay tamis mamatay,
Doon sa kasuyo ang abot ng araw;
Kamatayan pati ng simoy sa parang
Sa walang pag-ibig, ni ina, ng Bayan.
